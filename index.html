Prosthetic Arm with Haptic Feedback

One of the most meaningful technical experiences in my portfolio is my work with the Medical Robotics Club, where I have participated on engineering teams developing a prosthetic arm with haptic feedback intended to help restore the sense of touch. I am especially excited by this project because it reflects the kind of engineering that first drew me to bioengineering and electronic devices. Rather than treating engineering as a set of isolated equations or tools, this project shows how multiple technical systems can be integrated around a very human purpose: helping create more functional and responsive assistive technology.

The project environment has introduced me to a broad set of engineering processes and tools. Through my team participation, I have gained hands-on exposure to programming, microcontrollers, CAD, 3D printing, and machining. Each of these areas contributes to a different part of the overall system. Programming supports control logic and system behavior. Microcontrollers serve as the bridge between sensing, processing, and actuation. CAD enables the design of structural components that must fit together physically and function reliably. 3D printing and machining turn those designs into real parts that can be evaluated and improved. Even at this stage of my development, being part of a project that spans software, hardware, fabrication, and user-centered application has helped me better understand how engineering ideas become working prototypes.

What stands out to me most about this project is the emphasis on feedback and usability. Haptic feedback is important because it moves a prosthetic system beyond simple motion and toward a more responsive experience for the user. That design direction requires engineers to think carefully about sensation, interaction, and control rather than focusing only on mechanical movement. It also shows how electrical engineering can support accessibility and quality of life. Even when a project is technically complex, its success should still be judged by how well it serves the person using it.

This project has also strengthened my appreciation for collaboration. A prosthetic system is not built by one person working in one narrow specialty. It requires teammates who can contribute different technical strengths, communicate clearly, and adapt when a design needs to change. Being part of that environment has made me more aware of how much good engineering depends on teamwork, iteration, and attention to detail. For future employers, this experience demonstrates that I am motivated by challenging interdisciplinary problems and that I am eager to contribute in settings where engineering has a direct and visible impact on people’s lives.
